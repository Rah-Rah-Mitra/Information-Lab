# Image Rectification and Triangulation

*An overview of image rectification using the fundamental matrix and optimal 3D point triangulation through geometric error minimization.*

Image rectification and triangulation are critical processes in stereo vision for simplifying point matching and recovering 3D structure from 2D images.

## Image Rectification
[[Image Rectification]] is the process of resampling pairs of stereo images so that their [[Epipole]]s are mapped to infinity and the epipolar lines run parallel to the x-axis. This process relies on the [[Fundamental Matrix]] and applies a [[Projective Transformation]] to each image to reduce the stereo matching problem to a 1D search along horizontal lines.

### Rectification Procedure
1. Identify a seed set of image matches to compute the [[Fundamental Matrix]].
2. Find the [[Epipole]]s in both images.
3. Select a [[Projective Transformation]] that maps the [[Epipole]] to a point at infinity.
4. Find a matching transformation for the second image that minimizes the least-squares distance between corresponding points.
5. Resample both images according to these transformations.

## Triangulation
[[Triangulation]] is the method of computing the position of a 3D point given its projections in two views and the camera matrices. Because of measurement noise, back-projected rays are typically skew and do not intersect in space.

### Geometric Error Minimization
To find the optimal 3D point, the [[Maximum Likelihood Estimate]] (MLE) is used, which minimizes the [[Reprojection Error]]—the summed squared Euclidean distances between the projections of the estimated 3D point and the measured image points.

#### Estimation Methods
- **Linear Triangulation**: Uses the Direct Linear Transform (DLT) or inhomogeneous methods, though these are not always projective-invariant.
- **Sampson Approximation**: A first-order geometric correction that provides a computationally cheap approximation to the geometric cost function.
- **Optimal Polynomial Method**: A non-iterative algorithm that finds the global minimum of the reprojection error by solving a sixth-degree polynomial.

## Plane-Induced Geometry
When points lie on a world plane, their images are related by a [[Homography]]. A [[Homography]] is compatible with the [[Fundamental Matrix]] if the matrix $F H$ is skew-symmetric.

### Plane Induced Parallax
[[Plane Induced Parallax]] occurs when a 3D point $X$ is not on the plane $\pi$. The image of $X$ and the image of the point on the plane mapped by the [[Homography]] will be distinct but collinear on the epipolar line. This parallax can be used to determine the [[Epipole]] and subsequently the [[Fundamental Matrix]].

## Affine Approximation
In cases where cameras can be approximated as affine, the [[Affine Fundamental Matrix]] is used. This simplifies the geometry as all epipolar lines become parallel and the [[Epipole]] resides at infinity.
