# Computer Vision Bibliography and Research Trends

*This document provides an extensive bibliography of foundational and contemporary research in computer vision, covering topics from classical edge detection to deep learning. It serves as a comprehensive reference for researchers studying motion estimation, 3D reconstruction, and semantic segmentation. The list highlights the evolution of mathematical foundations and neural network architectures in the field.*

This note provides a high-level overview of the research landscape in [[Computer Vision]], as evidenced by the extensive bibliography provided in the source text. The field is a multi-disciplinary domain that integrates mathematics, statistics, and computer science to interpret visual data.

## Concept
[[Computer Vision]] is the scientific study of how computers can gain a high-level understanding of objects, scenes, and motion from digital images or videos. The research documented in the bibliography covers several core pillars:

- **Low-Level Vision**: Includes [[Edge Detection]] (e.g., Canny, 1986) and [[Optical Flow]] (e.g., Black & Anandan, 1993), which focus on the fundamental processing of pixel-level information.
- **Mid-Level Vision**: Involves [[Motion Estimation]] and [[Structure from Motion]] (e.g., Bolles et al., 1993), which aim to recover geometric properties and movement from sequences of images.
- **High-Level Vision**: Encompasses [[Semantic Segmentation]] (e.g., Chen et al., 2018) and [[Deep Learning]] (e.g., Bengio et al., 2009), which focus on object recognition, scene understanding, and the semantic labeling of pixels.
- **Geometric Reconstruction**: Focuses on [[3D Reconstruction]] (e.g., Curless & Levoy, 1996) and [[Camera Calibration]] (e.g., Brown, 1971), which are essential for mapping 2D image coordinates to 3D world space.
- **Image Restoration**: Includes [[Image Inpainting]] (e.g., Bertalmio et al., 2000) and denoising techniques, which are critical for preparing raw sensor data for higher-level analysis.
