# Krylov Subspace Projection Methods

*Krylov subspace methods are iterative solvers for large linear systems that use projection techniques to find approximate solutions. These methods include FOM, GMRES, and Lanczos, each differing in their orthogonalization strategies and computational costs. The choice between full and incomplete orthogonalization determines the balance between robustness and efficiency.*

This note explores the family of Krylov subspace projection methods used to solve large-scale linear systems of the form \(Ax = b\). These methods approximate the solution within a subspace generated by powers of the matrix acting on an initial vector.

## Concept

[[Krylov Subspace]] methods seek an approximate solution \(x_m\) by imposing a projection condition. For the [[Full Orthogonalization Method]] (FOM), the Galerkin condition is used, which requires the residual to be orthogonal to the subspace. For the [[Generalized Minimum Residual Method]] (GMRES), the residual norm is minimized over the entire subspace, providing a more robust but computationally more expensive approach.

In the [[Arnoldi Process]], an orthonormal basis for the Krylov subspace is constructed. This process often employs the [[Modified Gram-Schmidt]] orthogonalization to maintain stability. However, as the dimension of the subspace increases, the computational and memory costs grow significantly. To mitigate this, one can use the [[Incomplete Orthogonalization Method]] (IOM) or the [[Direct Incomplete Orthogonalization Method]] (DIOM), which truncate the orthogonalization to save resources.

The [[Lanczos Algorithm]] is a specialized version of these methods, particularly for symmetric or structured matrices, which builds a biorthogonal system rather than a full orthonormal basis. It is highly efficient in terms of storage but more susceptible to breakdown.

### Mathematical Formulations

In GMRES, the approximate solution is found by minimizing the residual norm:

$$ \min_{x \in \mathcal{K}_m(A, r_0)} \| r_0 - Ax \|_2 $$

This minimization is is often solved via a least-squares problem involving a [[Hessenberg Matrix]].

In the [[Lanczos Algorithm]], the biorthogonality of the vectors is maintained through a recurrence relation, resulting in a tridiagonal matrix structure rather than a full Hessenberg matrix.
