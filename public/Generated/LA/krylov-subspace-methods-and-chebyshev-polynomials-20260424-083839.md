# Krylov Subspace Methods and Chebyshev Polynomials

*This note explores the convergence properties and algorithmic variations of Krylov subspace methods, including GMRES, FOM, and Conjugate Gradient. It highlights the role of Chebyshev polynomials in analyzing convergence rates, particularly in the complex plane. The discussion also covers block Krylov methods for handling multiple right-hand sides.*

This note summarizes the key concepts of [[Krylov Subspace Methods]] used for solving linear systems, focusing on convergence analysis and algorithmic variations. 

## Concept
[[Krylov Subspace Methods]] are iterative methods that search for an approximate solution within a subspace spanned by the vectors $v, Av, A^2v, \ndots, A^{k-1}v$. Common algorithms include [[GMRES]] (Generalized Minimal Residual), [[FOM]] (Full Orthogonalization Method), and the [[Conjugate Gradient]] method. 

### Convergence and Chebyshev Polynomials
One of the primary tools for analyzing the convergence of these methods is the use of [[Chebyshev Polynomials]]. Specifically, the [[Chebyshev Polynomials]] of the first kind, $T_n(x)$, are used to bound the error or residual norm. For a positive definite matrix $A$, the [[Conjugate Gradient]] algorithm's error after $k$ steps is bounded by: 

$$ e_k = 2 \left( \frac{\sqrt{\kappa(A)} - 1}{\sqrt{\kappa(A)} + 1} \right)^k e_0 $$

This bound depends on the ability of a polynomial to minimize the maximum modulus on a certain interval or ellipse in the complex plane. [[Chebyshev Polynomials]] are asymptotically optimal for such problems. 

### Block Krylov Methods
To improve efficiency, especially when dealing with multiple right-hand sides, [[Block Krylov Methods]] are used. These methods, such as [[Block Arnoldi]] and [[Block GMRES]], operate on a group of vectors rather than a single vector. [[Block Arnoldi]] generates an orthonormal basis for the block-Krylov subspace.
