# Deep Convolutional Neural Networks

*An exploration of the architecture, forward and backward propagation, and training of Convolutional Neural Networks for image pattern classification.*

A [[Convolutional Neural Network]] (CNN) is a specialized neural network architecture designed for image pattern classification by leveraging spatial hierarchies of features.

## Architecture

### Convolutional Layers

In a [[Convolutional Layer]], the primary operation is convolution. A [[Receptive Field]]—a small neighborhood of pixels—is moved across the input image or previous feature map. At each location, a sum of products is formed between the pixels in the receptive field and a set of weights arranged in a [[Kernel]]. The number of spatial increments by which the receptive field moves is called the [[Stride]].

To generate a [[Feature Map]], the result of the convolution is summed with a bias and passed through an [[Activation Function]]. To ensure the same feature is detected at all points in the image, the same weights and bias are used for all locations of the receptive field; this is known as [[Weight Sharing]].

### Pooling Layers

Following the convolution and activation, [[Pooling]] (or subsampling) is used to reduce the spatial resolution of the feature maps, which helps achieve translational invariance and reduces the volume of data. This process occurs in a [[Pooling Layer]], where a [[Pooling Neighborhood]] is subdivided and replaced by a single value. Common [[Pooling]] methods include:

- [[Average Pooling]]: replaces the neighborhood with its average value.
- [[Max-Pooling]]: replaces the neighborhood with the maximum value.
- [[L2 Pooling]]: replaces the neighborhood with the square root of the sum of squared values.

## Classification and Vectorization

Because the outputs of the convolutional and pooling layers are 2-D arrays, they must be converted into vectors to be processed by a [[Fully Connected Neural Net]]. This process, known as [[Vectorization]], typically uses linear indexing to concatenate the 2-D arrays into a single column vector.

## Training and Optimization

CNNs are trained using [[Backpropagation]], where the error is computed at the output and propagated backward to update weights and biases. In the reverse direction, pooled feature maps are upsampled to match the size of the original feature maps.

### Challenges in Training

- [[Vanishing Gradients]]: occurs when gradient values become so small that gradient descent ceases to be effective, often in very deep networks.
- [[Overfitting]]: occurs when the network performs well on training data but fails to generalize to unseen test data. This can be mitigated by artificially enlarging the training set or using [[Dropout]], a technique that randomly removes nodes and their connections during training.
